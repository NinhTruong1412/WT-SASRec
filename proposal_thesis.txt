# Master's Thesis Proposal Strategy: Sequential Recommendation Systems

## Your Data Assets

You have a strong setup for a **recommendation system** thesis:

- **Sequential watch history** → captures user behavior over time
- **Watch time per content** → implicit feedback signal (engagement quality)
- **Content metadata** → enables content-based and hybrid approaches

---

## Recommended Thesis Framing

> *"Improving sequential recommendation by incorporating implicit feedback signals (watch time) and content metadata"*

This is focused, achievable in a master's timeline, and has clear novelty potential.

---

## Research Strategy (Step by Step)

**1. Start with baselines**
Implement standard sequential models first so you have something to beat:
- **GRU4Rec** – RNN-based session recommendation
- **SASRec** – Self-attention based sequential model
- **BERT4Rec** – Bidirectional transformer for recommendation

**2. Identify the gap**
Most sequential models treat all interactions equally. Your watch time data lets you ask: *"Does a user who watched 90% of a film signal stronger preference than one who watched 10%?"* — this is your research angle.

**3. Propose your method**
Introduce watch time as a **weighting or gating mechanism** into a baseline model. This is your core contribution.

---

## 3 Simple Research Directions

### Direction 1 — Watch-Time Weighted Sequential Model ⭐ (Easiest)
Modify SASRec or GRU4Rec to weight attention scores by normalized watch time. Items watched longer get higher influence on the next prediction.

- **Novelty**: Simple but underexplored
- **Effort**: Low-medium
- **Risk**: Low

### Direction 2 — Metadata-Enriched Sequential Recommendation
Enrich item embeddings with content metadata (genre, duration, language, cast) using a side-information encoder, then feed into a sequential model.

- **Novelty**: Well-studied but you can combine with watch time
- **Effort**: Medium
- **Risk**: Low

### Direction 3 — User Interest Drift Detection
Use watch time patterns over time to detect when a user's taste **shifts** (e.g., from action to documentary). Model short-term vs. long-term interest separately.

- **Novelty**: More original
- **Effort**: Medium-high
- **Risk**: Medium
